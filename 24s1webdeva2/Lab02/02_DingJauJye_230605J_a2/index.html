Electrical Safety Tips and Resources

About Us

The Electricity Safety Foundation is dedicated to promoting electrical safety in homes, schools, and workplaces.

We provide resources, education, and advocacy to prevent electrical injuries and deaths.

Common Hazards

Understanding common electrical hazards is the first step towards preventing accidents. Some common hazards include:

  • Exposed wires: Exposed wires pose a serious risk of electric shock and should be immediately addressed.
  • Overloaded circuits: Overloading circuits can cause overheating and lead to electrical fires.
  • Wet conditions near electrical sources: Water is a conductor of electricity and can cause electric shock if it comes into contact with electrical sources.
  • Damaged insulation: Insulation protects wires from exposure; damaged insulation can lead to short circuits and fires.
  • Uncovered electrical outlets: Especially dangerous for children, uncovered outlets can lead to electric shock.
  • Improper use of extension cords: Using extension cords as a permanent solution can lead to overheating and fires.
  • Faulty appliances: Appliances that are not functioning properly can pose electrical hazards and should be repaired or replaced.
  • Electrical arcing: Arcing can occur when electrical connections are loose or corroded, leading to sparks and potential fires.

Preventive Measures

Here are some measures you can take to ensure electrical safety:

  • Always use insulated tools: Insulated tools prevent electric shock when working with electrical components.
  • Regularly check and replace damaged wires: Damaged wires should be promptly repaired or replaced to prevent hazards.
  • Keep electrical devices away from water: To prevent electric shock, ensure that electrical devices are kept dry.
  • Use Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters (GFCIs): GFCIs can prevent electric shock by shutting off power when a fault is detected.
  • Install Arc Fault Circuit Interrupters (AFCIs): AFCIs help prevent fires by detecting and interrupting arc faults.
  • Don't overload outlets: Avoid plugging too many devices into a single outlet to prevent overheating.
  • Use appropriate wattage: Ensure that light bulbs and appliances match the recommended wattage for sockets and outlets.
  • Unplug unused devices: Unplugging devices when not in use can prevent electrical fires and save energy.
  • Schedule regular electrical inspections: Have a licensed electrician inspect your home's electrical system periodically.
  • Educate children about electrical safety: Teach children not to touch electrical outlets, cords, or appliances.
  • Use childproof outlet covers: Install covers to prevent children from inserting objects into electrical outlets.
  • Avoid DIY electrical work: Hire a licensed electrician for repairs and installations to ensure safety and compliance with codes.
