Singapore's Eletricity

Current Electricity Sources

In order for Singapore to power the homes and facilities of its citizens, the country relies on multiple sources to produce or obtain electrical energy. Interact with the images below to find out more about each source of electricity.

Coal

Sinagpore only has one coal-burning utility plant which is the Tembusu Multi-Utilities Complex on Jurong Island. It supplies steam and electricity to chemical companies on the industrial island.

Natural Gas

Singapore started to import piped natural gas from Indonesia and Malaysia in the early 2000s. From May 2013, the country then started to import liquified gas and stores them in storage tanks which were built on Jurong Island.

Solar Energy

Singapore is one of the most solar-dense cities in the world, with many solar panels installed both on the rooftops of HDB flats and in open spaces and reservoirs just like the 60 megawatt-peak floating solar photovoltaic system at Tengah Reservoir, which is equivalent to 45 football fields in size.

Waste to Electrical Energy

Singapore Collects trash everyday and transports incinerable waste to waste-to-energy(WTE) plants. The waste is stored in large refuse bunkers before being fed to incinerators which operate at temperatures of between 850 and 1,000 degrees Celsius. Steam generated from the heat will drives steam turbine generators in WTE plants to produce electricity. Learn more by watching the video.

Fuel/Crude Oil

Since Singapore has no crude oil deposits,the country imports it for refining and petrochemical industries. Over two-thirds of Singapore’s crude oil imports come from the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait. Most of Singapore’s refined petroleum product and petrochemical are exported to neighboring Asian countries.

Future Energy Plans

Singapore has laid out comprehensive plans and strategies to transform its energy sector towards sustainability and resilience, addressing both environmental challenges and economic opportunities.

Energy plans

  • Energy Reset (Green Plan 2030)

    According to Singapore's Green Plan, the aim is to deploy at least 2 gigawatt-peak worth of solar panels by the year 2030. The amount of energy generated from this is enough to meet the yearly electrical needs of around 350K households.

    Learn More Here

  • Decarbonising The Power Sector

    According to the Energy Market Authority, power generation accounts for about 40% of Singapore’s emissions as of 1st March 2024, with 95% of electricity being generated from natural gas. To decarbonise the power sector, Singapore will import electricity from other countries like Malaysia and explore low-carbon alternatives such as hydrogen.

    Learn More Here

  • Energy Imports

    Singapore has long-term agreements with neighbouring countries to start importing lower-carbon electricity. For example on September 8th 2023, conditional approval was given by Singapore's Energy Market Authority for the import of 2 GigaWatt of electricity from Indonesia. Companies including keppel Energy will begin to import 300 megawatts of it at the target start date of 2027 through a shared subsea transmission cable system.
